01 — The Case for Undergraduate Research

Why Research Transforms Students

Undergraduate research is one of the most powerful high-impact educational practices available to college students. Studies consistently show it increases confidence, builds scientific identity, and dramatically improves rates of graduate school enrollment — especially for first-generation and underrepresented students.

"91% of students who participated in an undergraduate research experience reported that it solidified or increased their interest in postgraduate education."

— PMC Study on Undergraduate Research Experiences (UREs), National Library of Medicine

"UREs increase interest in STEM careers for over two-thirds of students who participate, and 88% improve their understanding of how to conduct research."

— NSF-funded study of 4,500 undergraduate researchers (PMC11287787)

What PhD Admissions Committees Look For

  • Hands-on research experience — ideally with a faculty mentor producing measurable results
  • A personal statement that tells a specific scientific story, not a generic one
  • Strong letters of recommendation from researchers, not just course instructors
  • Evidence of intellectual curiosity: conference presentations, posters, publications
  • Familiarity with lab techniques relevant to the program you're applying to

What MD & MD-PhD Programs Look For

  • Research experience demonstrates commitment beyond clinical shadowing
  • MD-PhD programs specifically require research publications or presentations
  • AMCAS and AACOMAS both have dedicated research activity sections
  • A clear narrative connecting your research to your medical goals is essential
  • Programs like NIH SIP, U-RISE, and MARC are explicitly designed to build this pipeline

Key Takeaway: Research Can Replace Your Part-Time Job

A student holding a CRSP fellowship ($5,000/yr) plus an NSF REU ($6,000 summer) earns $11,000 in a single academic year while gaining research experience that directly improves their medical or PhD application. That is comparable to or greater than many part-time campus jobs — and it builds a future instead of just paying the present.

07 — Taking Action

How to Get Into Research Starting Now

01

Email a Professor

Go to your department's faculty page. Find someone whose research interests you. Send a 3-paragraph email: who you are, why their research interests you, and what you are asking for. Attach your transcript.

02

Apply to CRSP or CIRE

Applications open each spring. Minimum GPA 2.7 for CRSP. Identify a faculty mentor first — most applications require one. Your campus Research Office can help you find one.

03

Target External Deadlines

NSF REU: November–February. NIH SIP: March 1. DOE SULI: rolling. NASA OSSI: rolling. Start applying 3 months before any deadline. Apply to 5–10 sites to maximize your chances.

04

Present Your Work

CUNY hosts symposia every semester. Even a poster presentation on early results counts on your application. Conferences build your network, your confidence, and your CV simultaneously.

Common Myths — Busted

  • Myth: "I need a 4.0 to get into research." Reality: CRSP requires only a 2.7 GPA. Many programs evaluate potential over grades.
  • Myth: "Research is only for seniors." Reality: Start in your first year. Early experience compounds.
  • Myth: "I can't afford to do unpaid research." Reality: Virtually all programs listed here are paid. Research can replace a part-time job.
  • Myth: "I have to be a STEM major." Reality: CRSP, CIRE, and many others fund social science, humanities, and arts research.
  • Myth: "External programs are out of reach for CUNY students." Reality: CUNY students win NSF REU, NIH, DOE, and NASA positions every year.

08 — A Student's Journey

From QCC to the International Stage

To make this concrete, here is one example of what the CUNY research pathway can look like — a two-year journey that started with a single lab, and led to an international conference, funded graduate programs, and biomedical research at the CUNY School of Medicine.

🔬

Jan 2024 — QCC Physics Lab (Dr. Rex Taibu)

Radiation detection training using GM tube, NaI(Tl) detector, and ionization chamber. Environmental radioactivity experiments, sealed-source studies — the foundation of everything that followed.

🏆

Fall 2024–Spring 2025 — CRSP Fellowship ($5,000)

Full comparative study: Play-Doh, Super Flex bolus, and honey as radiation attenuation materials. Norman L. Hill Award. Phi Theta Kappa All-NY Academic Team. Presentations at QCC and Queens-Wide symposia.

🌍

Summer 2025 — DIA 2025 Global Annual Meeting, Washington D.C.

Presented research internationally alongside PhD candidates from Oxford and other top universities. Featured in QCC News. One of the only undergraduates selected among graduate-level presenters.

🧬

Fall 2025–Present — TS³ ($7,000) + CUNY School of Medicine

Transfer to CCNY, Biology major. Joined Dr. Mallikaratchy's molecular biology lab studying nucleic acid therapeutics. Total research funding received: $12,000+ across CRSP and TS³.

This journey illustrates each theme of this presentation: research building career clarity, preparing for graduate school, opening international opportunities, and providing real financial support through fellowships.
